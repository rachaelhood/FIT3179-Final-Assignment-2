Sharks, Shores & Survivors

Exploring where, when, and how shark attacks occurred in 2018-2019, across countries, activities, ages, and species, with context from population size.

Shark Attacks by Country

Use the slider to switch between 2018 and 2019. Each country is shaded on a blue scale by the number of recorded shark attacks in that year, darker = more incidents. Values are aggregated by country, so nations with long, surf-active coastlines may appear darker. Hover to see the exact country and count.

What the map shows

In 2018 shark attacks were heavily concentrated in a few key coastal regions, as shown on the map above. The darkest blue areas, Australia and the United States, recorded the highest number of incidents, reflecting their long coastlines and high levels of ocean activity. Moderate numbers occurred in Brazil, South Africa and New Zealand, while most other countries reported few or none. Overall, the map highlights how shark attacks in 2018 were unevenly distributed , clustering in warm, coastal environments where both sharks and humans are most active.

In 2019, shark attacks again clustered in a few coastal nations. The United States and Australia show the darkest blues, indicating the highest incident counts, with smaller but noticeable activity in South Africa, Brazil, Mexico, and New Zealand. Much of Europe and Asia remain pale or blank, reflecting minimal reports. Overall, the 2019 pattern mirrors 2018, where attacks are unevenly distributed, concentrated where warm-water coastlines and high ocean use overlap.

What were people doing?

Shark Attacks by Activity Type

In 2018, most shark attacks occurred during high-contact water activities. Surfing saw by far the most incidents, followed by swimming and fishing. Smaller numbers were linked to activities like spearfishing, wading and diving. This suggests that the greater the time spent in surf zones or near the water’s surface, the higher the likelihood of encountering a shark.

In 2019, most shark attacks occurred during surfing, followed by swimming and fishing. Smaller numbers were linked to snorkeling and spearfishing. Overall, attacks were most common in surface-level water sports, where people spend more time in active ocean areas.

Age x Country x Population

Comparing Shark Attack Frequency by Age and Country Population

In both 2018 and 2019, shark-attack incidents cluster overwhelmingly at one attack per country-age group, with smaller secondary clusters at two, and only occasional points at three, mainly in high-exposure countries. The modal ages sit in the late-teens to 30s, consistent with the group most active in surf and open-water sports, 2019 shows a slightly wider spread into the 40-60+ ranges. Bubble size (population) highlights the United States and to a lesser extent Australia as the largest contributors to total incidents, reflecting exposure rather than per-capita risk. Overall, the pattern is stable across years, attacks concentrate where coastal populations and ocean recreation are high, with age skewed toward active adult cohorts.
